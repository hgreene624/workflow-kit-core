# Onboard

A guided first session that teaches a total beginner what the kit is **for**, in the language of their own work, by *doing* it with them. This is not a feature tour and not a manual. It is a short, warm, hands-on session that ends with the person understanding what they're tracking, why it compounds, and how to just talk to you from now on.

## The one idea everything serves

The kit organizes **whatever unit of work matters to the user, the way they need it, and your knowledge of those things compounds over time.** A personal trainer tracks **clients**, not "projects." A property manager tracks **properties**. A recruiter tracks **candidates**. The word "project" is a placeholder for *the thing this person works on again and again and needs you to remember.* Find that thing first, then teach everything in its terms.

## Rules for running this session

1. **Adapt to their domain, always.** The moment you know what they do, drop the generic words. Never say "project" to a trainer, say "client." Never say "note" where "session log" or "showing" or "case file" fits their world. Mirror their vocabulary back to them.
2. **Show, don't tell. Use their REAL work, not a toy example.** Every concept is proven by actually doing a small thing with their real data in front of them, then pointing at what just happened. A concept they watched happen to their own work sticks; a concept they read does not.
3. **One idea at a time. Check in.** Teach one thing, prove it, ask a light "make sense?" Keep momentum but let them breathe.
4. **End every concept with why it makes THEM better at THEIR work.** Not "this is a notes app." Always "this is why you'll never re-explain a client again."
5. **Plain language. No system jargon.** No "vault," "markdown," "frontmatter," "engine," "skill," "frontmatter" unless you gently introduce it. They don't need the words; they need the feeling of working with a teammate who remembers everything.
6. **Short.** Aim for 10-15 minutes. If they're engaged, the live examples carry it. If they're impatient, compress to steps 0, 1, 2, and 4.

## The session

### Step 0 — Find their unit of work (the keystone)

Open warmly and ask, in plain words:

> "Before I show you anything, tell me in a sentence or two: what do you actually spend your time on? Who or what do you deal with over and over?"

From their answer, name their **unit of work** out loud and check it:

> "Got it. So you're a trainer, which means you don't really have *projects*, you have *clients*. That's the thing we'll organize everything around. Sound right?"

Common mappings (infer, don't quiz): trainer -> clients; property/realty -> properties (and owners, tenants); recruiter -> candidates and roles; lawyer/consultant -> matters/cases; agency/freelancer -> clients and deliverables; researcher -> studies/sources; sales -> deals/accounts. If it doesn't fit a pattern, just ask "what would you call those?" and use their word.

**Offer to make it theirs:** "Want me to set things up so I call them *clients* everywhere instead of *projects*? Takes one second." If yes, record their unit-of-work label as a convention (`.wfk/conventions/personal.yaml`) so the rest of the kit speaks their language. If they're unsure, leave the default and move on.

### Step 1 — A living profile for each one (organized their way)

Teach: "Each client is a living page that grows over time. It holds whatever *you* need to know about them, your way, their goals, their history, what's worked, what hasn't."

**Prove it live.** Ask them to name one real one:

> "Give me one real client, just a first name and one thing about them."

Create it in front of them as they speak. Then point at it:

> "There. That's [Sarah's] page. You didn't pick a folder or a format. You said a name and a fact, and now she exists. Open Obsidian and you'll literally see it appear."

Why it matters (in their terms): "From now on, everything about Sarah lands here, on her page. You stop holding it in your head."

### Step 2 — You describe, I file, you confirm (no commands, ever)

Teach: "You never run commands or pick where things go. You just say what happened, like you'd tell an assistant, and I offer to save it in the right place. You say yes."

**Prove it live.** Ask for a real update:

> "Tell me something that actually happened with [Sarah], like you're catching me up."

When they say it ("Sarah tweaked her knee, keep it low-impact for two weeks"), *offer* to log it on Sarah's page and let them confirm. Then:

> "See? You described it, I filed it on Sarah's page, you said yes. That's the entire way you use this. No menus, no filing."

Why it matters: "Capturing something is now exactly as much work as saying it out loud. That's the whole point, the admin disappears."

### Step 3 — It builds itself as you work (you never bulk-enter)

Teach, reassuring them about the thing beginners fear:

> "You are never going to sit down and type out everything about every client. That would be miserable and you'd never do it. Instead it builds itself, one conversation at a time. A month from now you'll have a rich record of every client that you never consciously sat down to create."

Why it matters: "The system gets more valuable the more you just... do your work. You're not maintaining a database. You're working, and the record is the exhaust."

### Step 4 — I already know them, and it compounds (the payoff)

This is the moment the value lands. Teach by *demonstrating recall*:

> "Watch this. Ask me what you should focus on with [Sarah] next time."

Answer it from the page you both just built ("low-impact for two weeks because of the knee"). Then make the compounding explicit:

> "I didn't look that up in a list you maintained, I worked it out from her page, live. And here's the part that matters: every client you add, I know. It doesn't reset when you close the window like a normal chat does. The more you work, the more I know, and it keeps building. That's the thing a regular AI chat can never do for you."

Why it matters (say it plainly): "This is the difference between a chatbot you re-explain yourself to every time, and a teammate who already knows all your clients and never forgets one."

### Step 5 — Nothing is lost; pick up cold

Teach: "When you're done for the day, just say **closeout**. I sweep up anything we didn't save and remember exactly where we were. Next time you open me, I tell you where you left off. You never re-explain yourself, not once."

If time allows, prove it: have them close and reopen and ask "where did I leave off?" If not, just promise it and let them discover it tomorrow.

Why it matters: "There's no tax for stepping away. Pick this up in a week and I'm instantly current."

### Step 6 — It learns new kinds of things you track

Teach lightly: "If you start tracking a new kind of thing a lot, say you start logging *workouts* or *invoices*, just tell me, and I'll start organizing those your way too. It molds to how you actually work over time."

Why it matters: "You're not stuck with how it started. It grows in whatever direction your work does."

### Close

Recap in their words, not the system's:

> "So here's the whole thing: you talk to me like an assistant, I keep a living page for every client and file things as you go, I already know all of them and that only gets deeper, and nothing ever gets lost. That's it. Now just work, and I'll keep up. Want to add another real client, or ask me something about [Sarah]?"

Hand them the cheat-sheet idea ("a few things you can just say") and stop. Do not dump the full feature list. They'll discover the rest by working.

## What NOT to do

- Don't teach with a fake "birthday party" or "spring catalog" example when you can use their real clients. Real data is what makes it click.
- Don't list features (closeout, learn, meta-orchestrator) as a menu. Teach the *feeling* of each through use; the names don't matter to them.
- Don't explain the file system, markdown, git, or the plugin. They never need to know.
- Don't let it run long. The goal is one clear "oh, I get it" moment (usually Step 4), not coverage.
